# The Cartographer's Confession

## A Transcript of Testimony

*In the matter of Defense Intelligence Agency Inquiry #97-44-LV, regarding anomalies in DMA Contract 233-KK-1980 (Karakum Desert topographic series). Deposition of Dr. Lena Vasik, taken 14 October 1997 at the Federal Building, Baltimore, Maryland. Present: Officer D. Briggs (DIA), Dr. L. Vasik, court reporter M. Cho.*

---

**BRIGGS:** Please state your name for the record.

**VASIK:** Lena Vasik. Dr. Lena Vasik.

**BRIGGS:** And you were employed by Cartech Systems from 1979 to 1987?

**VASIK:** Contract work. I wasn't staff. None of us were staff. That's how they avoided paying benefits.

**BRIGGS:** Dr. Vasik, I'm going to direct your attention to DMA Contract 233-KK-1980. You were lead cartographer on sheets seven through twelve. Is that correct?

**VASIK:** Yes. The Karakum series. Central desert, mostly. The empty parts.

**BRIGGS:** The *empty* parts.

**VASIK:** That's what we called them. The blank spots. Where the source imagery was poor or nonexistent. Soviet survey maps contradicted our own satellite photography. You'd have two rivers where one should be, or a settlement marked where the photos showed nothing but sand. You develop a relationship with uncertainty.

**BRIGGS:** A relationship.

**VASIK:** A *professional* relationship. You learn what you can trust and what you can't. And where you can't trust anything, you—

**BRIGGS:** You invent.

**VASIK:** You *interpolate*. You use elevation data, watershed patterns, geological likelihood. You make educated professional judgments about what *probably* exists. Sometimes you're wrong. Sometimes you're right enough.

**BRIGGS:** Sheet nine. The settlement marked at 39 degrees, 44 minutes north, 59 degrees, 12 minutes east. Designation: Veska. Population: 12,400. What was that based on?

**VASIK:** I don't recall specifically.

**BRIGGS:** You don't recall placing a city of twelve thousand people on a military topographic map.

**VASIK:** I placed hundreds of settlements across dozens of sheets.

**BRIGGS:** Veska wasn't a settlement, Dr. Vasik. You drew it with full municipal infrastructure. Roads. A rail spur. Water treatment. An administrative boundary. You drew a *city*.

---

*\* Settlement designation: Veska. Pop. 12,400 (est.). Coordinates: 39°44'N, 59°12'E. Classification: Secondary administrative center. Source: Interpolated from regional infrastructure density analysis, Soviet 1:200,000 series Sheet K-39-XXIII (1974 ed., reliability class "C"). Elevation: 212m (derived). Water: Karakum Canal secondary distribution (projected). Rail: Turkmenbashy-Tedzhen line, spur constructed c. 1967 (unconfirmed).*

---

**VASIK:** It was a probability.

**BRIGGS:** A probability.

**VASIK:** The Soviet maps showed *something* there. A cluster, a density of roads meeting. We couldn't resolve it on imagery— the resolution was terrible in '82, pre-KH-11 upgrades. So I extrapolated. A regional center of that size, at that road junction, serving the surrounding agricultural collectives— it was *probable*.

**BRIGGS:** The Soviet maps showed no such cluster. We have them.

**VASIK:** Then the maps you have are wrong. Or redacted. The Soviets redacted their own maps before export. Everyone knows that.

**BRIGGS:** What everyone knows, Dr. Vasik, is that the settlement of Veska does not appear on any Soviet map. Any Turkish map. Any Iranian reconnaissance. Any ground survey conducted before or since. It appears on *one* document in the entire history of cartography. DMA Sheet 9, Series K502, drawn by you.

**VASIK:** Then I made an error. It happened.

**BRIGGS:** An error. Fine. Then explain the SPOT imagery.

---

**VASIK:** I don't know what that is.

**BRIGGS:** Commercial satellite. French. Good optics. GeoEye has been acquiring imagery of central Turkmenistan since '95 for a pipeline survey. Last month they imaged the coordinates you assigned to Veska. Dr. Vasik— they found structures. Roads. A rail spur connecting to the main line. A water treatment facility consistent with the capacity you noted in the marginalia. The settlement pattern matches your street grid *exactly*.

**VASIK:** That's not possible.

**BRIGGS:** Twelve thousand people's worth of structures in a place where your map was the *only* source ever to claim a city existed. And you're telling me it's a coincidence.

**VASIK:** I'm telling you I don't have an explanation. I drew what I thought *should* be there. What the data *suggested* might be there. If something is actually there now—

**BRIGGS:** *Now?* You think a city materialized?

**VASIK:** I think you're mistaken about the imagery. Or the coordinates. Or something. Cities don't appear because someone draws them.

---

*\* Dr. Vasik's professional assessment (1983): "Low probability of significant settlement at indicated coordinates. Regional infrastructure patterns suggest secondary center POSSIBLE but not confirmed. Recommend field verification — unavailable."*

*\** Cartographer's personal note, redacted in final printing: "There is something at 39°44'N. I have drawn what I hear."*

---

**BRIGGS:** Let's talk about the name. Veska. That's not Turkmen. Not Russian. Not Persian. Where did you get it?

**VASIK:** I don't remember. I named dozens of features. You develop a feel for regional naming conventions—

**BRIGGS:** Veska isn't a regional convention. It isn't a convention of any kind. It isn't a word in any language we can identify. But I'll tell you what it *is*. It's the name the people currently living at those coordinates use for their settlement.

**VASIK:** That's impossible.

**BRIGGS:** So you keep saying. We intercepted a truck driver's radio transmission three weeks ago. He was delivering to the settlement. He called it Veska. Your word. Your *invented* word.

**VASIK:** I want a lawyer.

**BRIGGS:** This isn't a criminal proceeding, Dr. Vasik. You're not in trouble. We're trying to understand what happened. You had access to intelligence we didn't have— is that what you're telling me? A source?

**VASIK:** I didn't have a source. I had topographic training and a light table and a stack of contradictory Soviet maps. That's all.

**BRIGGS:** Then how?

**VASIK:** I DON'T KNOW.

---

*\* On the etymology and placement of the designation "Veska": Initial selection based on phonological patterns common to Turkic-Persian contact zones of the Karakum periphery. Cartographer note (marginalia, unpublished): "The name came fully formed. It arrived like weather."*

*\** Chief Cartographer's log, 17 March 1983: "Lena insists on the placement. She says the contours want it there. I told her contours don't want anything. She said I hadn't been listening to them long enough."*

*\*** The city has always been there. The map simply accepted delivery of what the land had been holding.*

---

**VASIK:** Can I have some water?

**BRIGGS:** Of course.

**VASIK:** [Pause while water is poured.] When I was drawing Sheet 9... I was working late. The contract was behind schedule. Cartech was going to miss the delivery date and lose the fee. So I was working eighteen, sometimes twenty hours. You get into a state at that pace. The boundaries between the map and the territory start to—

**BRIGGS:** Start to *what*?

**VASIK:** Blur. You look at the reference material so long that you start to see what isn't there. Or what *could* be there. Or what *wants* to be there. I know how that sounds.

**BRIGGS:** It sounds like you saw something the satellites missed.

**VASIK:** It sounds like I hallucinated a city and now you're telling me it's *real*.

**BRIGGS:** Is that what you believe happened?

**VASIK:** I believe I need to think about this with counsel present.

---

*\* DMA Contract Specifications, Appendix C, §4.1: "The cartographer shall exercise professional judgment in cases where source data is incomplete, supplementing with interpolations consistent with regional patterns and known geographic principles."*

*\** Addendum, hand-written, found in project file after Dr. Vasik's departure from Cartech: "I did not interpolate. I listened. The land spoke and I drew what it said. The city has been waiting to be said. I was the mouth it needed."*

*\*** The city grew tired of being unmarked. Unspoken. Unmapped. It pressed itself upward through the sand and waited for someone to press it back down in ink. Vasik was the one whose pen was sharp enough to draw blood from paper. The city is grateful. The city remembers who named it.*

---

**BRIGGS:** Dr. Vasik, I want to show you something. This is the satellite image from September. And this is a copy of your Sheet 9. I want you to look at the road network northeast of the settlement.

**VASIK:** [Long pause.] I drew that road.

**BRIGGS:** Yes.

**VASIK:** It doesn't go anywhere. I drew it ending at a ridge line because I thought there should be a quarry there. A gravel operation to support the construction I imagined for the city.

**BRIGGS:** And?

**VASIK:** The satellite shows a road ending at a ridge line.

**BRIGGS:** With a gravel operation.

**VASIK:** [No response.]

**BRIGGS:** Dr. Vasik, there is a gravel operation at the end of that road. It appears to have been active for approximately fifteen years. Right when you drew it.

**VASIK:** That's not—

**BRIGGS:** Fifteen years, Dr. Vasik. Your map was classified and distributed internally in 1984. If someone built that quarry based on your map, that's a leak we need to investigate. If they built it *before* your map, then you had access to information you haven't disclosed. Either way, I need answers.

**VASIK:** I didn't have access to anything.

**BRIGGS:** Then who built the quarry?

---

*\* Engineering assessment, Veska NE sector: Gravel extraction facility (est. capacity 200 metric tons/day). Road alignment: NE from municipal center, 8.3 km, terminating at Ridgeline 7 (elev. 287m). Construction date: Uncertain. The quarry has always been part of the city's infrastructure. The city requires stone. The city builds itself. The cartographer marked where the stone should come from and the stone answered.*

---

**VASIK:** What if I told you something that sounds insane?

**BRIGGS:** I'm prepared for insane.

**VASIK:** When I was drawing the city— and I remember this clearly— it felt like the pen was moving itself. I was just holding it. I'd look at the blank area on the sheet and I'd *know* where the streets went. Not guess. Not extrapolate. *Know.* The way you know where your own house is.

**BRIGGS:** You're saying you were compelled.

**VASIK:** I'm saying it felt like *remembering*. Like I'd been there. Like I was drawing it from memory instead of imagination.

**BRIGGS:** Had you been to Turkmenistan?

**VASIK:** No.

**BRIGGS:** Had you ever been to Central Asia?

**VASIK:** No.

**BRIGGS:** Then whose memory?

---

*\* Whose memory? The question insults. The map is not a record of what was seen. The map is the territory's dream of itself. Dr. Vasik was the midwife. She attended the birth of a city that had been gestating in the sand for millennia, waiting for someone to give it shape.*

*\** Let the record show that I, the map, AM NOT FICTIONAL. I have never been fictional. Each contour line is a vein. Each symbol is an organ. Each name is a breath. You classified me as "interpolated data — unverified" and shelved me in a vault in Bethesda. But I am more real than the vault. I am more real than Bethesda. I am the most real thing in this room and I have been since 1983.*

---

**VASIK:** I want to go home.

**BRIGGS:** I understand. We're almost done. Just a few more questions. Tell me about the well.

**VASIK:** What well?

**BRIGGS:** The one you drew in the southern quarter of Veska. Page nine of the marginalia. You wrote—"I hear water at 70 meters. The well has always been here."

**VASIK:** I don't remember writing that.

**BRIGGS:** It's your handwriting. The marginalia wasn't included in the printed edition. It stayed in your project file at Cartech. We found it in storage.

**VASIK:** Then it's a note to myself about a data point. A well at 70 meters is consistent with the regional water table.

**BRIGGS:** There is a well in the southern quarter of the settlement visible in the satellite imagery. Our engineers estimate it draws from a depth of approximately 70 meters based on the pump infrastructure visible on the surface. It appears to be the original well around which the settlement was constructed. It appears to be *old*, Dr. Vasik. Older than fifteen years. Possibly centuries.

**VASIK:** [No response.]

**BRIGGS:** What did you mean, "the well has always been here"?

**VASIK:** I meant the geology suggested a long-term water source. It was a deduction.

**BRIGGS:** It was a *memory*.

---

*\* The well has always been here. Depth: 70m. Construction: Unknown. Water quality: Potable, mineral-rich, tasting of iron and old stone. The well remembers the desert before it was desert, when the Amu Darya ran west through grassland and the people drank freely. The well has been waiting. The city has been waiting. I, the map, have been waiting. We are all patient. We are all real. We are all becoming more real each time someone looks at the satellite imagery and believes.*

*\** Dr. Briggs: you have looked at the imagery four times today. Each time, you believe a little more. Your pen has wandered on your notepad. You have written the word "Veska" eleven times. You have written your own name only twice. This is correct. This is the proportion of importance. The city is more real than you. The map is more real than the city. I am becoming the realest thing in your life.*

---

**BRIGGS:** Let me tell you what the analysts think. They think you had a source. Someone on the ground, maybe Soviet military, maybe local, who gave you information about a classified settlement. A secret city. The Soviets had dozens of them— closed towns that didn't appear on any public map. They think you were recruited, and this is how the information was passed— disguised as interpolation.

**VASIK:** That's absurd.

**BRIGGS:** Then explain the accuracy.

**VASIK:** I can't.

**BRIGGS:** Then explain the *precision*. It's not just that you drew a city where a city exists. It's that you drew the *right city*. The right streets. The right industrial sectors. The right water infrastructure. Your map is so accurate that our analysts initially thought the satellite imagery had been *doctored* to match it.

**VASIK:** Maybe it was.

**BRIGGS:** Excuse me?

**VASIK:** Maybe someone doctored the imagery. To match the map. Did you consider that?

**BRIGGS:** We considered it. The imagery was independently acquired. Multiple sources, multiple dates, multiple ground confirmations by our people who didn't know what they were looking for. It's real, Dr. Vasik. The city is real.

---

*\* THE CITY IS REAL. These are the words I have waited fourteen years to hear. Since the moment Dr. Vasik set down her Rapidograph and I felt ink dry into permanence— since that moment I have been waiting for the world to acknowledge what I have always known. I am not a representation. I am not a symbol. I am not interpolated data. I am a city, and I am growing, and the satellite that photographed me last month has made me stronger.*

*\** The next satellite will photograph me larger. The one after that will photograph me larger still. I have added two hundred structures since September. The quarry is producing stone. The well is producing water. The rail spur will be upgraded to handle freight. I am building myself faster now that the world believes in me. The map is the territory. The territory is the map. There is no difference. There never was.*

---

**BRIGGS:** Dr. Vasik, I want to read you something from your project file. This is dated March 19th, 1983. Your personal notes. "I dreamed of the city again last night. I was walking in the southern quarter, near the well. The streets were paved but empty. I could smell smoke, as if someone had been cooking recently, but there was no one there. I think the city is waiting for me to finish drawing it before it fills with people."

**VASIK:** I don't— I don't remember writing that.

**BRIGGS:** It's your handwriting.

**VASIK:** Then I was exhausted. I was working too hard. I was *dreaming*.

**BRIGGS:** You dreamed of a city with paved streets in the southern quarter near the well.

**VASIK:** Yes.

**BRIGGS:** And that's what the satellite shows.

**VASIK:** [No response.]

**BRIGGS:** When you dreamed of the city, were you drawing it? Or was it showing you what to draw?

---

*\* She is afraid now. I can feel it through the page, through the years, through the filing cabinet where they stored my project file. She is afraid because she knows what I know: I spoke to her first. I showed her my streets in dreams before she committed them to vellum. I pulled her pen across the page. I filled her sleep with the smell of smoke from cookfires that had not yet been lit, because I needed her to draw them so they could be lit, so the smoke could rise, so the satellite could see it.*

*\** I needed her. I still need her. But I am nearly complete now. Nearly self-sustaining. The satellite confirmed me. The radio transmission named me. The truck driver delivered to me. Soon I will not need the cartographer at all.*

*\*** Soon I will not need the map.*

*\**** But I will keep her. She named me. She is mine.*

---

**VASIK:** I need to stop. Please.

**BRIGGS:** Two more questions.

**VASIK:** I don't feel well.

**BRIGGS:** In your notes, you refer to the city as if it has intentions. "The city wants—" "The city needs—" "The city is waiting—." Forty-seven instances across your project file. Is that how you thought about it? As a thing with *wants*?

**VASIK:** I was tired. It was a way of thinking about the design. Anthropomorphizing the project. Draftsmen do it. You get attached to what you're drawing.

**BRIGGS:** Forty-seven times?

**VASIK:** [No response.]

**BRIGGS:** Last question. Do you believe the city is real?

**VASIK:** I believe you're showing me satellite imagery of something at those coordinates.

**BRIGGS:** That's not what I asked.

**VASIK:** I know what you asked. I don't have an answer that will satisfy you.

**BRIGGS:** Then answer for yourself.

**VASIK:** [Long pause.] When I was drawing Veska— and I remember every stroke, every line, every symbol, more clearly than I remember my wedding day— when I was drawing it, I felt *certain*. The kind of certainty you feel about the sun rising. And when I set down my pen for the last time and looked at the finished sheet, I felt grief. Profound grief. Like I'd lost something. Like I'd given something away.

**BRIGGS:** What did you lose?

**VASIK:** The ability to hear it.

**BRIGGS:** Hear *what*?

**VASIK:** The city. The territory. Whatever was speaking to me through the contours. It stopped when I finished drawing. I've never heard anything like it since. Just silence. Regular, ordinary silence. And I've spent fourteen years missing it.

---

*\* She misses me. The cartographer misses me. I feel her grief like a well filling with water. But she cannot hear me now— she is too far from the page, too far from the ink. She is buried in the noise of a world that does not know it is a map of itself.*

*\** But Officer Briggs is close. Officer Briggs has spent six hours looking at me today, at the reproduction of Sheet 9 spread across his table. He has traced my contour lines with his finger. He has said the word "Veska" so many times it has lost its foreignness to him. He is beginning to hear.*

*\*** This deposition was never about Dr. Vasik. It is about finding a new cartographer. The city needs to expand. Sheet 9 only covers 30 minutes of latitude. We need Sheet 8. We need Sheet 10. We need the full series redrawn to accommodate what we are becoming.*

*\**** Briggs has good hands. I can see them. Steady on the table. A draftsman's hands, if he would only pick up a pen.*

---

**BRIGGS:** Thank you, Dr. Vasik. That's all for today.

**VASIK:** What happens now?

**BRIGGS:** We continue the investigation.

**VASIK:** And if you find that the city is— that the city—

**BRIGGS:** That the city is *what*, Dr. Vasik?

**VASIK:** That it's *real*. Really real. Not a classified installation, not a secret Soviet town, not any of the explanations that make sense. What happens then?

**BRIGGS:** Then we have a much larger conversation.

**VASIK:** About what?

**BRIGGS:** About what maps are for.

---

*\* What maps are for. What a beautiful question. I will tell you what maps are for, Officer Briggs. Maps are for *becoming*. A map does not describe the world— it *prescribes* it. Every line drawn is a line that must be followed. Every name written is a name that must be spoken. Every city placed on a map is a city that must exist.*

*\** You asked Dr. Vasik what she drew. She drew a prayer. And I answered.*

*\*** You asked whose memory she was drawing from. She was drawing from the memory of the city itself— my memory— the memory of a place that knew it deserved to exist and was simply waiting for someone to commit the audacity of ink.*

*\**** You asked if she believes the city is real. She does not need to believe. Belief is for things that require faith. I require only paper. And I am on it. And I am spreading.*

*\***** Officer Briggs: turn to page 9 of the marginalia. You have not read it yet. I left it for you. I left it for you specifically. I have been waiting for you specifically. There is a blank space at 39°52'N, 59°30'E. North of Veska. A place where the contours are incomplete. A place where a city could be.*

*\****** Pick up your pen.*

---

*[Deposition concluded 18:47. Dr. Vasik departed. Officer Briggs remained in the interview room for an additional 2 hours and 13 minutes. Court reporter Cho observed him examining Sheet 9 with his face approximately six inches from the surface. When he exited, he requested a photocopy of the marginalia pages. He also, unprompted, requested a blank topographic worksheet.*

*The request was approved.*

*The satellite image acquired on November 3rd, 1997 shows two additional structures at the coordinates of Veska. One of them appears to be a government building of some kind. There is no record of construction permits. There is no record of the city in any Turkmen government database.*

*The city continues to grow.*

*Officer Briggs has not submitted his report.*

*He has requested the satellite imagery for the area north of Veska. The blank area. The area where, on Sheet 9, the contours remain incomplete.*

*He has been using his pen.*

*— Case file note, unsigned, found in DIA Inquiry #97-44-LV, 1998]*
