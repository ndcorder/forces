## Critic Review

This essay achieves something remarkably difficult: it invents a grammar of loss so convincing that the reader mourns features that never existed. The three linguistic constructs — the extinct case, the unwitnessed infix, the particle for feared outcomes — are worldbuilding of the highest order, each one a philosophical argument embedded in morphology. The essay's honesty about its own failures of recovery is its greatest structural virtue; the progression from "I played it seventeen times" through "the sentence does not resolve" to "I will not guess at their meanings" enacts the very deterioration it describes. The final line — "This is not what I wanted to say. It is what I have the words for." — is a perfect closure that refuses the consolation of meaning-making while nevertheless having made meaning. This is The Foundry at full strength: specificity that generates universal resonance without ever reaching for it.
