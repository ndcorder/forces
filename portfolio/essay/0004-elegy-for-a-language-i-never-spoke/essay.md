# Elegy for a Language I Never Spoke

## I.

My grandmother's diary is a school composition book, the kind with a stitched spine and grid-ruled pages. She kept it between 1961 and 1963, in a rented room above a shoe repair shop on ulica Bogusława in Szczecin. I found it in a plastic bag inside a suitcase inside a closet in her apartment in Cicero, Illinois, three months after she died. She had not marked it for preservation. It was not hidden. It was stored.

The diary is written in a language I cannot read.

I say this with more precision than it might seem to warrant. The language is not Polish, though my grandmother was fluent in Polish and used it for all outside communication for sixty years. The language is not German, though the village where she was born—Kamień, 1924—had been German for a century before the border moved in 1945 and it became Polish, and German again in the sense that the old people still whispered in it at church. The language she wrote in was what she called *kaszëbsczi*, and what linguists call Slovincian, a West Slavic language once spoken in a handful of villages along the Łebsko Lake, classified as extinct in the 1950s by the Polish state, though my grandmother apparently did not receive this news.

There are perhaps forty pages. Her handwriting is small and regular, a schoolgirl's hand. She underlined words she found important and drew small crosses in the margins next to entries about weather and money. I believe these crosses indicate days when she attended Mass, though I cannot confirm this.

## II.

I showed photographs of the pages to a linguist at the University of Chicago who specializes in endangered Slavic languages. He replied with excitement and then with frustration. He could identify the language but could not find anyone to translate it. The last semi-fluent speaker he knew of had died in 2012—a man in Gdańsk who had been a child during the war and retained only fragments, mostly prayers and the names for lake fish.

I began translating the diary myself, using a Kashubian dictionary (a related but distinct language), a comparative grammar of the Lechitic dialect continuum, and a set of field recordings made by a German linguist in 1929 that I found in an archive in Berlin. The recordings feature a woman's voice reciting vocabulary lists, and I will confess that I listened to them for longer than was necessary to complete the work. She said the word for *window* and I played it seventeen times. This is the kind of thing the essay form is supposed to make meaningful. I am not sure it is.

My translations are approximate. I want to be honest about the degree of error, because the essay form also tends to present approximation as recovery, and I want to resist that. When I write that my grandmother's diary entry for March 14, 1961 reads "The snow is dirty but will not leave," the word I have translated as *dirty* (*kùrno*) might mean *ashen* or *soiled* or simply *dark*. The word I have translated as *will not leave* (*zademieni*) appears to contain a prefix meaning *against* and a root meaning *to pardon*, which suggests something like *the snow will not grant release*, but I am projecting.

## III.

Here is what I can say with reasonable confidence about the language my grandmother wrote in, which I will call Slovincian for convenience though she never used that word:

It had seven noun cases and a separate case—marked by the suffix *-ële*—for things that no longer exist. The *extinct case*, as the Berlin linguist called it in his notes, was used for demolished buildings, deceased people whose names carried weight, and at least one dried-up lake. The word *wiesële* means not "the village" but "the village that is gone." My grandmother uses it often. I believe she was describing Kamień as it existed before the border shift.

It had a verb aspect—distinct from tense, mood, or evidentiality—for actions that the speaker has witnessed but the listener has not. The *-nów- infix* transforms *I carried the wood* (*jô dérwiwnãł*) into *I carried the wood and you did not see it but I need you to believe me* (*jô dérwiwnówãł*). This is not metaphorical. It is grammatical. The speaker is required by the structure of the language to distinguish between events that are shared knowledge and events that remain un-witnessed. I have not found a way to express this in English that does not sound like a lie or a plea.

It had a modal particle, *çe*, placed after the verb, which indicated that the action occurred as the speaker had feared it would. *Bëłë çe* does not mean *they were*. It means *they were, as I was afraid they would be*. My grandmother uses this particle on entries concerning her rent, her health, and the behavior of a woman named Dobrochna who I believe was her landlord's wife.

These features are not poetic inventions. I have reconstructed them from comparative analysis and field notes, and I have checked them against the one scholarly paper I could find, which was published in 1966 in a Poznań journal and consists of three pages and an apology for the brevity.

## IV.

Here is what I cannot recover:

The word *snôżëna* appears in entries from January and February of both years. The closest cognate in Kashubian means *beauty*, but my grandmother used it to describe ice conditions on the street outside her window. I believe it refers to a specific quality of frozen water—perhaps ice that has thawed and refrozen into a glaze too thin to support weight but thick enough to catch light. But this is reconstruction from context and climate. The word itself is gone.

The word *zómk* appears once, in an entry from August 1962, following a description of a fever. It appears to be related to the Polish *zamek*, meaning *castle* or *lock*, but neither translation makes sense in context. She wrote: *Bëłë çe zómk na pół noc.* *They were, as I feared, zómk toward midnight.* The sentence does not resolve. I have looked at it for many hours and it does not resolve.

The entry for September 3, 1962 contains twelve words I can translate and three I cannot. The three are *skrzeszewi*, *mùlëna*, and *dąbrowëca*. I will not guess at their meanings. I have decided that some things should remain blank, and this is one of them.

## V.

My grandmother did not teach her children the language. My father cannot say a single word of it. When I asked him about it once, he said: "She said it was a language for no one." She was probably right. By the time she arrived in the United States in 1971, the language had been functionally extinct for two decades. There was no one to speak it with. She switched to Polish and then to English and never returned to it in our presence.

But she wrote in it. She wrote in it every few days for at least two years, in a composition book, in a room above a shoe repair shop, in a city she had moved to because the borders moved first. She wrote about the snow and the rent and a woman named Dobrochna. She wrote *wiesële*, the gone village, and *bëłë çe*, it was as I feared, and *snôżëna*, which might mean ice glaze or beauty or something in between that I will never have the word for.

I do not feel haunted by this language. I want to be precise about that too. Haunting implies presence—the ghost in the hallway, the voice in the empty room. What I feel is more like the lack of a wall where a wall should be. An absence with structure. A doorway that opens onto the same room it was supposed to lead out of.

The diary sits on my desk. I have translated what I can. The rest is not a mystery waiting to be solved. It is a set of sounds a woman made on paper sixty years ago, sounds no one alive can correct or confirm or argue with. They are, for the first time since she wrote them, entirely hers.

This is not what I wanted to say. It is what I have the words for.
