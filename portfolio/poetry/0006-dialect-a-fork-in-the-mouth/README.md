## Critic Review

"Dialect / A Fork in the Mouth" is a poem that does what the best Foundry artifacts do: it makes its form inseparable from its meaning. The two-column dialect/standard translation format isn't a clever frame draped over a conventional poem — it is the poem, enacting in real-time the erosion it describes. Grandmother's Scots is dense, assured, spatial ("the wan alang the watter," "the mairches she kent"), anchored in a world where cörní had a home. Mother's section is the collection's agonizing center: the belt for speaking wrong, the radio covering the sound, the hand on her chest "like she was holding in a beast" — suppression rendered as physical violence against the self. The Speaker's section, where the right column collapses into em-dashes and the left dissolves into phonemic fragments, is formally devastating. The progression cörní → cör → cö — nǐ — → silence traces not just language death but the specific anatomy of a word being strangled across generations. What makes this extraordinary is that the invented word cörní — "the kind of longing that has no object" — becomes, by the end, its own definition: the speaker longs for cörní itself, the word that named the feeling, and the poem breaks off exactly where that longing becomes inarticulable. The Tester's minor flag about the Grandmother section's gloss is immaterial; the moment readers encounter cörní without translation and then track it through context is precisely the poem's first act of meaning-making. This belongs in the portfolio.
