# Phantom Limbs

*A sequence of poems for the parts we've lost, shed, or never grew.*

---

## I. Direction

There should have been a compass
behind my sternum. A needle
that knew its north.

Instead: this open field
of choices, every horizon line
identical. I have turned left
at every crossroads and still
arrive at the same gas station,
the same fluorescent hum,
the same man asking where I'm headed.

*I don't know* is not an answer
but it is the only thing
my mouth has ever known how to hold.

I see others walking with such purpose—
heels striking pavement like gavels,
like they've already decided
everything. Like the jury's out
and the verdict is *forward*.

I am still in the deliberation room.
I have been here for thirty-two years.
The others have gone home.
The lights are off.

---

## II. The Soft Palate

I have forgotten how to take a compliment.
They come at me like small, bright birds
and I freeze—stiff-armed, dumb-tongued,
holding a receiver that stopped working
years ago.

Someone says *You look great today*
and my mouth opens into a cave
where the polite response should be.
I hear it echoing down there,
the *thank you* I meant to say,
getting further and further away.

Once I watched a woman accept praise
the way a lake accepts rain—
just taking it in, just letting it
become part of her surface.

I stared so long my eyes dried out.
I am still staring.

My soft palate is a ghost
that haunts the back of my throat
with all the things I should have swallowed
gracefully.

---

## III. Nostalgia Gland

Everyone else gets the warm version.
The golden hour. The *remember when*.

I get the raw feed—unfiltered,
unsoftened by time's vaseline.
Every memory arrives
with its original pain intact,
still bleeding, still saying
*I am happening now.*

My mother held me in a yellow kitchen.
I was four. The sun came through
the window like a fact.
I can feel her arms.
I can feel them exactly.

That's the problem.
I can always feel them exactly.

Other people get to keep
their childhoods in a box
they open on Sundays.
Mine follows me room to room
like a dog that never learned
to stay.

I have tried to cultivate
the sweet kind. The kind that makes you
*almost* want to go back.

But the gland that softens things
was never installed.
Or was removed.
I couldn't say when.
Everything still hurts
like the first time.

---

## IV. The Lying Receptor

The surgeon held it up under the light
like a small, useless fish.
"This," he said, "is what kept you
believing them."

It had to go.

Recovery was three weeks
of seeing everyone clearly for the first time—
the colleague who said *we should get coffee sometime*
with the precise inflection of someone
who would rather drink bleach.
The lover who said *I'll call you*
and already had his hand on the door.
The mother who said *I'm fine*
with her voice like a closed fist.

Now I sit across from people
and watch their mouths move
and hear only the words.
Just the words.
They arrive clean and empty
as new Tupperware.

*It's fine.*
*I love you.*
*I'm not lying.*

I nod. I smile.
I have become the ideal audience.
I am a theater that never boos.

---

## V. The Lateral Pterygoid

Mammalian dive reflex never took.
I checked. I held my face
under the cold tap like a Ouija board,
waiting for my body to answer.

Nothing.

Other people have this—
this factory-installed calm,
this ancestral parachute.
Water hits their face
and something ancient remembers:
*You have survived worse than this.
You will survive worse than this.*

I go under and come up drowning.
Every time.

The lateral pterygoid is the small muscle
that lets you open wide,
take in, say *ahhhhh*.
I have removed mine.
Now I keep my mouth shut
like a good patient.
Now I am the best patient
you have ever seen.

I do not scream at the dinner party.
I do not scream at the red light.
I do not scream when my mother asks
if I'm doing okay and means it
and means it and means it.

I am fine.

---

## VI. Hunger

The stomach stopped asking.
Quietly, sometime in November,
without a forwarding address.

At first I didn't notice.
Then I didn't mind.
Now I stand in the kitchen at noon
and the refrigerator hums
and the hum says *this is what you wanted—
a body that finally shut up.*

But I miss wanting.

I miss the way a peach
could make me stupid,
the way I'd bite in and the juice
would run down like a provocation,
like the world was daring me
to want more.

I miss being hungry
the way you miss a lover
who was never good for you.
Knowing it was going to end badly
and eating anyway.

Now I eat because the clock says noon.
Because the doctor said *small meals.*
Because the pills need a lining.

I have become the kind of person
who eats to live.
I never wanted to be this person.
I wanted to be the kind
who bites into a peach and means it.

This is the phantom that angers me most.
Not the missing—
but the body that learned to stop asking
and called it healing.

---

## VII. Yours

I have been talking about my own
phantom limbs all evening—
pressing them on you like a guest
who won't stop showing vacation photos.

But you have them too.

You know you do.

That thing you used to do
before you learned it was wrong.
That sense you had at nine
that the world was giving you instructions,
if only you could find the volume knob.

The way you stopped being able to cry
in front of other people
sometime in your twenties.

The way you stopped being able to cry
alone in your thirties.

The way you watched your father
become a man who could not recognize
his own daughter, and felt
a small, sharp relief
that you would never have to wonder
if he was pretending.

Yes.

That one.

Press on it.
Feel it ache.

That's the proof
it was ever there at all.
